# Cold intro — Elementary literacy / instructional coach

**Recipient:** District-level elementary literacy coach, instructional
coach, or building-level reading/ELA coach (K–5 focus). NOT a principal
(see night 2 draft for that). NOT a classroom teacher (see night 1 ELA
draft for that).

**Context:** Literacy coaches are high-leverage because they (a) see
across many classrooms, (b) advise building leaders on adoption, and
(c) already think in terms of oral language as part of literacy. They
are underserved by oral-language tools — most edtech aimed at literacy
coaches is about decoding, phonics, or guided reading. A "reps for the
speaking and listening strands you already own" pitch lands in an
empty slot in their mental model.

**Why now:** End of April / start of May is the window when coaches are
planning next year's professional learning focus. They are not planning
next week. Pitching a summer pilot or a fall classroom pilot is more
credible than an "in the next two weeks" ask.

**Guardrails:**
- Do not imply we've done a pilot with their peers. We haven't.
- Do not claim COPPA-aligned, FERPA-aligned, SOC 2, or SDPC
  registration. We do not have any of those artifacts. Say "private by
  design; DPA on request before paid deployment."
- Lead with the Speaking & Listening standards, because that's the
  coach's language. Don't lead with AI.

**Subject line options** (pick one to personalize):
1. `Oral language reps for your K–5 teachers — quick note`
2. `A free 5-minute daily speaking drill aligned to SL.K–SL.5`
3. `Bright Speaker — 2 minutes of your time, no pitch`

## Draft

Hi [Name],

I run Bright Speaker, a new browser-based tool that gives K–5
students short, private speaking reps on a Chromebook. I'm writing
because literacy coaches keep telling us the same thing: the speaking
and listening standards (SL.K–SL.5) are in every scope-and-sequence
document, but very little instructional time actually lands on them.

Two minutes of context and then a concrete ask.

**What it is.** Students pick a prompt — show-and-tell, a book-talk
retell, an opinion sentence — and speak to their Chromebook for 60
seconds. The app surfaces filler words and eye-contact coaching in
real time, in kid-friendly language. The video never leaves the
device. There are no student accounts required during pilots; kids
launch with a class code.

**What it isn't.** It's not an assessment, it's not speech therapy,
and it's not a Flip replacement. The bet is simple: kids become
better speakers by practicing with feedback, and classrooms almost
never have the bandwidth to give that feedback 1:1.

**Where coaches fit.** You're who I'd want to evaluate this. If it's
a fit, we run a free 30-day pilot in one classroom in a building you
trust, and I sit down with the teacher and you on day 30 to review
what worked. If the 30 days didn't change a thing, we part as
friends and I've learned something. If it did, we talk about how
Bright Speaker could support your next professional-learning cycle
on oral language.

**Our honest state.** Bright Speaker is pre-launch. We don't yet
hold COPPA Safe Harbor attestation, SOC 2, or a published FERPA
opinion. Student video stays on the device, we minimize what we
collect, and we'll sign a DPA on your district's template before any
paid deployment. That's it, and we're transparent about it in every
procurement conversation.

Two classroom resources we wrote with coaches in mind, in case the
pitch doesn't hit but the content does:

- A 5-minute daily speaking warm-up you can run without any
  technology: https://brightspeaker.com/blog/five-minute-elementary-speaking-warmup
- A 2-minute classroom explainer on why pauses beat "um" — print
  or post: https://brightspeaker.com/blog/why-pauses-beat-um

**The ask.** If you have a building in mind where a teacher might
want to try this in May or early fall, I'd love a 20-minute intro
call. My calendar is wide open this week and next.

Either way, thank you for the work you do. Oral-language instruction
is an under-resourced part of elementary literacy, and the teachers
I've talked to who still invest in it are the ones whose students
carry it with them.

Warmly,
Brandon
Founder, Bright Speaker
hello@brightspeaker.com
