# Draft — Elementary literacy / instructional coach cold intro

**Recipient:** Elementary literacy coach, instructional coach, or ELA
coach (K–5 or PreK–5) at a public or independent district. Typical
titles: "Elementary Literacy Coach," "K–5 ELA Coach," "Instructional
Coach — Elementary," "Literacy Specialist."
**Context / why now:** K–5 is the primary wedge (decision 0002).
Principals are the budget-holder lane, teachers are the free-tier
adopter lane, and **coaches are the multiplier lane** — they influence
dozens of classrooms per building and care specifically about
standards-aligned oral-language practice. Completing the K–5 ICP trio.
**Tone:** Peer-to-peer, standards-forward, practical. A coach is
already drowning in vendor pitches; this should read like a teacher-
facing colleague who happened to build a tool, not a sales email.
**Status:** Draft. Brandon reviews, personalizes, and sends.

---

**Subject line options (pick one per recipient):**

- A printable SL.K–SL.5 warm-up for your coaching rotation
- Oral language without the grading pile — for your K–5 teachers
- Five minutes of speaking practice, standards-aligned

---

**Body:**

Hi [Coach first name],

I'm [Brandon / a colleague at Bright Speaker]. I'm reaching out
because you coach K–5 teachers on literacy, which means you probably
already know the thing most curricula dodge: the speaking-and-
listening standards are real, but nobody has time for them.

We built Bright Speaker for exactly that gap. It's a 5-minute,
Chromebook-based speaking warm-up that students run on their own. One
60-second rep per student per day, aligned to Common Core SL.K–SL.5,
with kid-appropriate feedback on eye contact, pace, and filler words.
Nothing for the teacher to grade. No video leaves the device.

Two things that might be useful to you this week, whether you ever
look at our product or not:

1. A printable K–5 warm-up routine that fits in a morning meeting —
   works with or without technology:
   [link: /blog/five-minute-elementary-speaking-warmup]

2. A 2-minute &ldquo;Why pauses beat um&rdquo; explainer with a
   classroom handout — the thing we teach before we teach anything
   else about filler words:
   [link: /blog/why-pauses-beat-um]

Both are free to share with the teachers you coach — I wrote them so
you could hand them out, not funnel people to us.

**One ask:** if it lands with one or two of your teachers and
you'd like to see what a 30-day classroom pilot looks like, I'd
love 20 minutes on your calendar. Pilots are free; the point is to
learn, and we want coaches in the room because you know what's
actually running in these classrooms.

Thanks for the work you do.

[Brandon Johnson / Founder, Bright Speaker]
hello@brightspeaker.com · brightspeaker.com

**Internal notes for Brandon:**

- Coaches are a relationship game. One personalized note per day,
  batched by state or district, will outperform a mass send 10x.
- If possible, name the adopted literacy curriculum in the opener
  (Benchmark, EL, HMH, Wit & Wisdom, CKLA, Fundations, etc.) — it
  signals you understand the building, not just the grade band.
- The lead-with-resources posture is intentional: this email has
  two printable links before it has a CTA. That's the difference
  between a pitch and a peer note.
- If they reply positively, the right next step is a 20-minute
  coach-to-coach call, not a demo. Demos lose coaches; workflow
  fit wins coaches.
- If they forward to a teacher, count it. Teacher-from-coach
  referrals are the highest-quality pilot lead we can get.
- Don't claim COPPA/FERPA-compliant. "No video leaves the device"
  with plain-English specifics is the honest line.
